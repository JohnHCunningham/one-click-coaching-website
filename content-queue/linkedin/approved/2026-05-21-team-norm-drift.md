## Final Revised Post

Nobody told her the methodology was optional.

The team showed her.

She came out of training sharp. Upfront contracts on every call. Pain questions that went three layers deep. She was doing the work.

Then she looked around.

Nobody else was.

Same training. Same certification. Same Sandler. But on live calls, the rest of the team had drifted back to what felt easier. Rushed discovery. Shallow qualification. Demos that started before the buyer had earned one.

She held on for a month.

Then the pressure arrived. A deal slipped. A manager asked about the forecast. And the gap between what she was doing and what everyone else was doing started to feel more expensive than the gap between the methodology and her results.

Within eight weeks, she stopped using the upfront contract.

Stopped digging for deeper pain.

Started doing what the team did.

Not because the training failed. Not because she lacked discipline. But because being the only person following the playbook in a room where nobody else does is not sustainable.

Training drift is not just a memory problem.

It is a social problem.

When the team norm says the methodology is optional, even the best-trained rep eventually conforms. And unless reinforcement hits the whole team — consistently, call after call — the culture wins.

It always does.

Drop a comment if you've watched this happen — a rep who arrived sharp from training and, within a quarter, you could not pick them out of the rest.

## Suggested First Comment

The forgetting curve gets most of the attention in training ROI discussions.

But in my observation, the social force is stronger than memory decay.

One rep executing the methodology alone — while the team norm says it's optional — faces a pressure that no workshop prepares them for.

The question for sales leaders is not just whether individual reps remember the training. It is what the team is actually tolerating on live calls — and whether reinforcement is hitting everyone, not just the newest hire.
