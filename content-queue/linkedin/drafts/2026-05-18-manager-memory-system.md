## Original Post Draft

A sales manager with 12 reps cannot be the memory system for the methodology.

Not because they do not care.

Because the math breaks.

Twelve reps.
Eight calls a week.
Dozens of moments where pressure takes over.

A skipped upfront contract.
A shallow pain question.
A rushed demo.
A next step that sounds clear but is not real.

The manager may hear one call and coach it well.

But next week, the same rep drifts in a slightly different way.
Another rep repeats a pattern from two weeks ago.
A third improves, then slides back when the deal gets tense.

That is not a discipline problem.

It is a memory problem.

Methodology reinforcement cannot depend on one person remembering every rep's last call, last coaching note, last weakness, and next correction.

Managers should coach.

But they should not have to be the whole operating system.

The bottleneck is not intent.
It is bandwidth.

## Suggested First Comment

The useful question for sales leaders is not "are managers coaching?"

It is: "does the coaching system remember what each rep needs next?"

## Image Prompt

Create a clean infographic showing "12 reps x 8 calls/week x 30 minutes review = 48 hours" in large type. Below it, show a lone sales manager calendar filled with blocks labeled forecast, pipeline review, hiring, 1:1s, escalations, coaching. Style should be minimal, executive, muted, with plenty of whitespace. Tone: practical, not humorous. Include the line: "The bottleneck is not intent. It is bandwidth."

## Why This Should Appeal

This speaks directly to a sales leader's lived constraint: they believe in coaching, but they cannot manually observe, remember, and reinforce every methodology behavior across every rep. It avoids blaming managers and reframes undercoaching as a system-design problem. That creates demand for OCC's category without pitching the product too early.
