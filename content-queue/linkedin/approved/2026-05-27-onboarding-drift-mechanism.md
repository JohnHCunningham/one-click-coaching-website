He was the best new hire they'd made in two years.

Sharp. Curious. Took notes during the methodology training. Asked the kind of questions that made the trainer pause.

First month: he ran the qualification framework exactly as taught. Every discovery call had structure. Every deal stage had clear exit criteria.

Month two: still solid. A few shortcuts here and there, but nothing that raised flags.

Month three: nobody noticed.

Because by month three, his calls sounded like everyone else's. The discovery questions got shorter. The qualification framework got fuzzy. The exit criteria became "they seemed interested."

He didn't forget the methodology.

He just stopped using it.


Nobody reinforced it.

His manager was managing 11 other reps. Reviewing calls meant picking two per month, not two per week. The coaching happened in quarterly reviews, not after every call.

The gap wasn't the training. The gap wasn't the talent. The gap wasn't the effort.

The gap was a system that treats onboarding as the finish line instead of the starting line.

Most sales teams onboard the rep and hope the methodology sticks.

The teams that win onboard the rep and build a loop that makes it impossible to drift.

Drop a comment if you've seen this pattern — and how long it usually takes before someone notices.

## Suggested First Comment

This isn't a hypothetical. I've watched it happen across organizations using Sandler, Challenger, SPIN — the methodology doesn't matter. What matters is whether anyone is reinforcing it after week four.

The best new hires don't fail because they can't learn. They fail because the system assumes learning equals retention — and it doesn't. Retention requires repetition. Repetition requires a loop. Most teams have brilliant onboarding and zero reinforcement architecture.

Curious how others have tried to solve this. Have you built anything that actually closes the gap after onboarding ends?
