You can measure training attendance.

You cannot measure training execution.

Your team spent $40,000 on methodology training last year.

You know who showed up. You know who passed certification. You know the NPS scores were strong.

But if someone asked you today — "Is the methodology actually showing up on your reps' calls?" — what would you point to?

Not attendance records.
Not certification scores.
Not rep feedback.

None of those measure behavior.

The gap isn't that your team didn't learn the methodology.

The gap is that nobody can prove they're still using it.

CRM stages won't tell you.
The rep advanced the deal to "proposal." The CRM logged it. But did they run the Up-Front Contract? Did they qualify the pain? Did they confirm budget?

The CRM doesn't know. It records outcomes, not behaviors.

Even conversation intelligence — which gets closer — stops at talk patterns and sentiment.
It sees the conversation.
It doesn't see the methodology.

So one of your largest enablement investments operates without a single behavior-level metric.

Attendance is measured.
Execution is assumed.

And when the CFO asks what that training actually delivered, "the reps said it was great" won't hold.

Most sales leaders can't prove their training is being used on live calls.

The ones who can won't have to fight for their next budget.

Curious if anyone's actually tried to measure this.

## Suggested First Comment

The uncomfortable question most enablement leaders avoid: if your CFO asked you to prove — with behavior data, not satisfaction data — that your last training investment changed how reps execute on calls, could you?

I've asked this in discovery conversations for months. Almost no one can answer. Not because they don't care. Because the tools to score methodology execution at scale haven't existed.

Would be curious to hear from anyone who's found a way to close this gap.
