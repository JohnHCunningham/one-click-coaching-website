### LinkedIn Angle 1: The Undercoached Middle

**Target Persona:** VP Sales or Sales Manager who invests coaching time in the top rep (to retain) and the bottom rep (to fix) — while the 60% in the middle get nothing.

**Pain:** You know coaching matters. You do it. But when you look at who actually gets coached, it's the squeaky wheels — not the reps with the most room to grow.

**Hook:** "The most expensive rep on your team isn't the one missing quota."

**Structure:** Scene → reveal → insight → landing line. Open with a specific image of a manager reviewing a coaching log and noticing the middle of the distribution is a ghost town. Reveal that this isn't a prioritization failure — it's a structural inevitability when one human selects who gets coached from memory. Land on: the middle is where the money sits.

**Core Insight:** The top rep gets coached because you want to keep her. The bottom rep gets coached because you have to fix him. The eight reps in the middle — the ones who could gain 15% each — get ignored because they're not on fire and they're not about to leave. The distribution of coaching is the opposite of the distribution of opportunity.

**Why It Matters Now:** Budget pressure is rising. Headcount is frozen at most B2B companies. The only lever left is getting more from the reps you already have — and the middle is the only untapped source of revenue uplift.

### LinkedIn Angle 2: The Arithmetic of Coaching Coverage

**Target Persona:** VP Sales who keeps telling managers to "coach more" and can't figure out why it doesn't happen.

**Pain:** You've said it. Your managers have heard it. Coaching remains inconsistent. You assume it's a motivation problem. It's not.

**Hook:** "It's time you stopped measuring coaching effort. Start measuring coaching arithmetic."

**Structure:** Provocation hook → numbers → paradox → landing line. Lead with a stark number: 480 calls per month, 20 reviewed. Then the paradox: managers aren't failing to coach because they don't want to. They're failing because the math makes meaningful coaching coverage impossible without a system. This isn't a people problem — it's an infrastructure problem.

**Core Insight:** A sales manager with 10 reps × 12 calls/week = 480 calls/month. Review 2 calls per rep = 20 calls reviewed = 4.2% coverage. The other 96% of calls generate zero feedback. That's not a coaching failure. That's a design failure. No amount of manager training or exhortation changes the arithmetic.

**Why It Matters Now:** Sales leaders are being asked to improve rep performance without adding headcount. The math says that without automation, coverage is mathematically impossible. The only answer is a coaching infrastructure layer.

**OCC Pillar:** Data / Neuroscience (Round 1 — establishes the structural gap)

**Image Prompt:** A chalkboard with the equation "480 calls ÷ 1 manager = 4% coverage" written in white chalk. The equals sign is emphasized. Below it, in smaller writing: "The manager isn't the problem. The math is." Espresso chalkboard, Bone chalk.

### LinkedIn Angle 3: Pressure Reveals What Got the Most Reps

**Target Persona:** Sales Enablement leader who watched reps nail the methodology in role-play and watched it evaporate on live calls — and needs language for why.

**Pain:** You've seen the gap. You can't explain it to leadership without sounding like you're blaming the training or the reps. Neither is the problem.

**Hook:** "Pressure doesn't reveal who someone is. It reveals what they've practiced the most."

**Structure:** Scientific reframe → scene → consequence → landing line. Open by challenging the common saying. Under pressure, people don't rise to the occasion — they revert to whatever pattern their nervous system has run the most times. The rep who nailed the framework in role-play practiced it 8 times. The pattern of "talk more, qualify less" has been practiced for years. On a live call, the older pattern wins.

**Core Insight:** Behavior under pressure isn't a function of intention or training. It's a function of repetition count. The framework practiced 8 times in a workshop cannot compete with a default pattern practiced for years. The only fix is more reps with corrective feedback — hundreds of them — tied to real calls, not role-plays.

**Why It Matters Now:** Every sales leader is asking "why didn't the training stick?" The neuroscience answer is: it did stick. The problem is that 8 repetitions of the new behavior can't overwrite thousands of repetitions of the old one without a reinforcement loop that runs after every call.

**OCC Pillar:** Neuroscience / Data (Round 1 — explains the mechanism without naming OCC)

### Blog Angle 1: Coaching Infrastructure — Why Sales Is the Only High-Stakes Discipline Without One

**Target Persona:** CRO or VP Sales who has invested in people, training, and tools — but still feels like the coaching function is bolted on instead of built in.

**Pain:** You've bought the CRM. You've bought the CI tool. You've paid for methodology training. But coaching still happens ad hoc — when the manager has time, when a deal goes sideways, when a rep asks. It doesn't happen as infrastructure.

**Hook:** "Every high-stakes discipline on earth built coaching into the workflow. Except yours."

**Structure:** Open with comparison — sports (every athlete has multiple coaches, video review after every performance), medicine (grand rounds, case review, peer observation), aviation (checklists, simulators, debriefs). Then the contrast: in sales, coaching is a calendar item. A QBR slide. A quarterly role-play nobody takes seriously. Build the case that coaching infrastructure isn't a nice-to-have — it's what separates consistent performers from lucky ones. Land on: the systems exist. The question is whether you'll build the infrastructure or keep hoping managers find the time.

**Core Insight:** The problem isn't that sales managers don't want to coach. It's that coaching was never designed into the workflow. Every other discipline with life-or-death stakes — sports, medicine, aviation, military — has coaching embedded in process. Sales, a discipline where millions of dollars ride on individual conversations, has nothing. No infrastructure. No embedded feedback loop. Just a manager and a calendar invite.

**Specificity elements:** Sports coaching ratios (1:5 to 1:10 in pro sports vs 1:8 to 1:12 in B2B sales), medical residency model (observed practice with immediate correction), aviation debrief protocol. Contrast each with the equivalent in sales — or its absence.

**Why It Matters Now:** AI has made coaching infrastructure possible at a price point that works for 5-50 rep teams. The window is open. Companies that build the infrastructure layer now will have a structural advantage over those that don't.

### Blog Angle 2: The 4% Problem — Why Your Coaching Sampling Rate Guarantees Drift

**Target Persona:** VP Sales or Sales Enablement leader who tracks coaching activity (sessions held, reps covered) and thinks they're doing enough — but isn't tracking the ratio of calls coached to calls run.

**Pain:** You run coaching reports. You see activity. Managers are doing something. But performance isn't moving. The answer is in a number you've never calculated: your coaching sampling rate.

**Hook:** "If your team runs 480 calls a month, and your managers review 20 of them, you're sampling at 4%. The other 96% of calls — the ones nobody reviews — are where your training investment is slowly eroding."

**Structure:** Open with the 4% number. Build the case that this is a structural inevitability, not a manager failure. Walk through the math for a typical 10-rep team. Then contrast what the 4% catches vs what it misses: the rep who abandoned the framework in month 2 isn't in the 4% because she's not visibly failing — she's slowly drifting. By the time she lands in a reviewed call, the pattern is set. Close with: the only way to close the sampling gap is to make every call coachable without requiring a human to review every call.

**Core Insight:** The coaching sampling rate is the single most important metric nobody tracks. It determines whether your training investment compounds or decays. At 4%, decay is guaranteed — the rep who's drifting won't get caught until the pattern is hardened. At 100% — every call scored automatically, with humans reviewing only the exceptions — reinforcement becomes possible. The math is unforgiving, but it's also the clearest business case for coaching automation.

**Specificity elements:** Real math: 10 reps × 12 calls × 4 weeks = 480 calls. Manager capacity: 2 calls/rep/month = 20 calls. Sampling rate: 4.2%. What happens in the other 460 calls: patterns form uncorrected. Actual cost example: one rep with a qualification gap that goes uncorrected for 3 months costs $45K in deals that stall at proposal stage.

**Why It Matters Now:** Budget season is approaching. Every VP Sales will be asked to justify their enablement spend. The sampling rate is the number that makes the case for automation — it's not about doing more. It's about coverage that's mathematically impossible without a system.
