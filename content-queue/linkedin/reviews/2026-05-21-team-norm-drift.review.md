## Advisor Pass

### 1. What is Working

- The scene opener is immediate: "She came out of training sharp." You can see her. The reader is inside the story from the first line.
- The pivot from individual drift to cultural dynamics is this post's strongest contribution. No other post in the May 18–21 cadence addresses the social contagion of methodology abandonment.
- Short, rhythmic paragraphs. Sparse voice throughout.
- The landing line "the culture wins every time" lands with weight and finality.
- "She stopped because she was the only one using it" — aphoristic, memorable, quotable.

### 2. What is Weak

- "And here is the thing nobody talks about" is a weak pivot. It announces the insight instead of delivering it. Replace with a show-don't-tell turn.
- Only Sandler is named. For broader appeal, a second methodology reference would help without overclaiming — "Same Sandler. Same certification" could become "Same Sandler training. Same Challenger cohort. Same MEDDIC workshop" to show the pattern is universal.
- The CTA is good but could be sharper. "Drop a comment if you've seen this" is generic. Better: invite a specific memory.
- Missing one concrete time marker — "within eight weeks" or "by the end of the quarter" — to anchor the drift timeline.

### 3. What the Audience Will Care About

Sales leaders have watched this exact thing happen: a rep comes in hot from training, then the team absorbs them. They've felt helpless about it. They've blamed the rep, the training, or themselves. This post names a force they've felt but never articulated: the team norm is a stronger teacher than any workshop. That recognition will create resonance — and curiosity about what could counteract it.

### 4. What Should Be Sharpened

- Replace the pivot phrase entirely.
- Add a time marker for the drift.
- Broaden methodology reference slightly (still no overclaim).
- Tighten the CTA to invite a specific memory.

### 5. Stronger Hook Options

A. "Nobody told her the methodology was optional. The team showed her."
B. "She came out of training sharp. Within eight weeks, you could not pick her out of the team."
C. "The training worked. The team didn't."

### 6. Stronger Central Argument

Methodology drift is not just a memory problem or a bandwidth problem. It is a social problem. Reps conform to the lowest common denominator of behavior the team tolerates. One rep trying to execute the methodology alone — while everyone else wings it — faces a choice: stay lonely in their discipline or blend in. Most blend in. The training investment dies not in the individual but in the culture. Unless reinforcement is team-wide and consistent, call after call, the social norm devours the workshop.

### 7. Stronger Close Options

A. "The culture wins. It always does — unless you build something stronger."
B. "Drop a comment if you've watched a rep arrive sharp and leave the methodology behind — not because they forgot, but because the team norm was stronger than the training."
C. "Reinforcement cannot be individual if the drift is cultural."

## Final Revised Post

---

Nobody told her the methodology was optional.

The team showed her.

She came out of training sharp. Upfront contracts on every call. Pain questions that went three layers deep. She was doing the work.

Then she looked around.

Nobody else was.

Same training. Same certification. Same Sandler. But on live calls, the rest of the team had drifted back to what felt easier. Rushed discovery. Shallow qualification. Demos that started before the buyer had earned one.

She held on for a month.

Then the pressure arrived. A deal slipped. A manager asked about the forecast. And the gap between what she was doing and what everyone else was doing started to feel more expensive than the gap between the methodology and her results.

Within eight weeks, she stopped using the upfront contract.

Stopped digging for deeper pain.

Started doing what the team did.

Not because the training failed. Not because she lacked discipline. But because being the only person following the playbook in a room where nobody else does is not sustainable.

Training drift is not just a memory problem.

It is a social problem.

When the team norm says the methodology is optional, even the best-trained rep eventually conforms. And unless reinforcement hits the whole team — consistently, call after call — the culture wins.

It always does.

Drop a comment if you've watched this happen — a rep who arrived sharp from training and, within a quarter, you could not pick them out of the rest.
