## ADVISOR PASS

### 1. What's Working
- **Hook is a clean antithesis.** "You can measure training attendance. You cannot measure training execution." Seven words each. Instant tension. The reader knows exactly where this is going.
- **The "what would you point to?" pivot** is the strongest structural moment. It moves the reader from passive recognition to active self-examination.
- **Asyndeton in the dismissal sequence** creates speed and finality: "Not attendance records. Not certification scores. Not rep feedback." Each line dismisses a metric the reader has been relying on.
- **CRM and CI paragraphs** build the systemic argument without over-attacking. They're presented as incomplete tools, not bad investments. Smart positioning for an audience that likely owns both.
- **The CFO closing** converts intellectual problem into career-relevant urgency. Budget defense lands harder than training philosophy.
- **Landing line** is strong: "The ones who can won't have to fight for their next budget." Quiet authority. No chest-thumping.

### 2. What's Weak
- **Missing anchor number.** The brief had "$40,000 in Sandler training" and "$2,500–3,000/rep." Using one of these early makes the cost concrete. Without it, "heavily" is vague.
- **The "largest enablement investment" line** is slightly overbroad. Not every reader considers training their largest enablement line item. Could soften to "one of your largest."
- **No CTA.** The post ends with a strong landing line but no invitation to engage. A single comment-prompt line ("Curious if anyone has actually tried to measure this.") would open the door without feeling salesy.
- **Middle section rhythm.** The CRM and CI paragraphs are structurally similar — both follow "X won't tell you. Here's why." The second instance risks feeling repetitive. Could vary the structure of one.

### 3. What the Audience Will Care About
- **Budget defense.** The CFO question is the emotional core. Sales leaders fight for training budgets. Being unable to prove ROI is a vulnerability they feel.
- **The measurement gap itself.** Many leaders sense this problem but haven't named it. The post gives them language: "The gap is that nobody can prove they're still using it."
- **Competitive positioning.** The landing line ("the ones who can won't have to fight") implies that measurement capability is a competitive advantage among peers. That's a status threat — effective and ethical.

### 4. What Should Be Sharpened
- Add a concrete dollar figure in paragraph 3: "Your team spent $40,000 on methodology training last year." (or "$2,500–3,000/rep")
- Vary the CRM/CI structural pattern. Keep CRM as "CRM stages won't tell you. [Example]." Flip CI to: "Even conversation intelligence — which gets closer — stops at talk patterns and sentiment. It sees conversation. It doesn't see methodology."
- Add one comment-prompt line after the landing line: "Curious if anyone's found a way to measure this — drop a comment."
- Tighten "So your largest enablement investment" to "So one of your largest enablement investments."

### 8. Final Revised Post

---

You can measure training attendance.

You cannot measure training execution.

Your team spent $40,000 on methodology training last year.

You know who showed up. You know who passed certification. You know the NPS scores were strong.

But if someone asked you today — "Is the methodology actually showing up on your reps' calls?" — what would you point to?

Not attendance records.
Not certification scores.
Not rep feedback.

None of those measure behavior.

The gap isn't that your team didn't learn the methodology.

The gap is that nobody can prove they're still using it.

CRM stages won't tell you.
The rep advanced the deal to "proposal." The CRM logged it. But did they run the Up-Front Contract? Did they qualify the pain? Did they confirm budget?

The CRM doesn't know. It records outcomes, not behaviors.

Even conversation intelligence — which gets closer — stops at talk patterns and sentiment.
It sees the conversation.
It doesn't see the methodology.

So one of your largest enablement investments operates without a single behavior-level metric.

Attendance is measured.
Execution is assumed.

And when the CFO asks what that training actually delivered, "the reps said it was great" won't hold.

Most sales leaders can't prove their training is being used on live calls.

The ones who can won't have to fight for their next budget.

Curious if anyone's actually tried to measure this.

### 9. Suggested First Comment (revised)

The uncomfortable question most enablement leaders avoid: if your CFO asked you to prove — with behavior data, not satisfaction data — that your last training investment changed how reps execute on calls, could you?

I've asked this in discovery conversations for months. Almost no one can answer. Not because they don't care. Because the tools to score methodology execution at scale haven't existed.

Would be curious to hear from anyone who's found a way to close this gap.
